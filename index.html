Milestones in Technology

The First Computer

Faster Than A Man?

The Electronic Numerical Integrator And Computer, better known as ENIAC was the first large-scale “computer” to run at speed no human could. It is described as “a collection of electronic adding machines and other arithmetic units, controlled by a web of large electrical cables,” says David Grier, IEEE Annals of the History of Computing. It ran more calculations than all of mankind at that point.

In 1943-1946 at the Moore School of the University of Pennsylvania the ENIAC was constructed. The principal designers, J. Presper Eckert, an electrical engineer at the Moore School, and John Mauchly, a physicist who became interested in calculating devices from statistical methods. The purpose of this super computer was to be a quick computing machine designed to compute ballistic tables within a short amount of time. However, the war ended before the machine was fully operational, but was able to solve a problem for the hydrogen-bomb project.

WiFi

Faster Than Light?

WiFi has played a major role in keeping us connected at home and in the public. We regularly rely on WiFi to maintain our productivity, organization, and some even use it for protection and health. WiFi Access Points are set up at home and in many local places. It gives internet access to everything ranging from laptops to tablets to smartphones. WiFi first hit the scenes in in 1997 created by a group called 802.11. As the years progress, it has advanced into a stronger and faster speed that covers more distance than the earlier versions.

It is more than just simply going on online to browse social media or surf the web, it has also made multiple electronics become interconnected and exchange information back and forth. Undeniable, WiFi is no longer a one way street. We now connect in our personal and professional lives. It is constantly improving our efficiency and communication and pressing against the boundaries of what’s next to come in the future.

iPhones

The New and Bigger Step

Less than a century ago, one of the most famous computer incorporations was founded. It was founded on April 1st, 1976 by two dropouts, Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak. Apple, the company who had a vision of changing the way people viewed computers. Apple’s first smartphone called the ROKR E1 was released on September 7th 2005. This was the first mobile phone to use iTunes (a music sharing software Apple created prior, in 2001.) The ROKR was an Apple and Motorola collaboration, but Apple discontinued the ROKR because they felt like they could make a better project. On January 9th 2007 the first iPhone was born at the Macworld Convention, it was soon available for costumers on June 29th 2007.

The new iPhones were such a success because they were more advanced than any other competition. Apple created the first smartphone that was entirely touchscreen that broke new technological ground with its multitouch controls. Setting the stage for the future, almost all mobile devices are touchscreen. iPhones are primarily remembered as the product that set Apple on its route to success but they didn’t stop there. So advanced, they were able to produce other devices such as iPads, airpods, and other useful things that benefit the society.

Pixar

Animations Yay

Pixar Animation Studios is a motion-picture studio subsided from the Disney Company. It originated in the 1970s at the New York Institute of Technology, also known as, NYIT. There a team of elite computer scientists contributed to the emerging field of computer graphics. Over time the division developed the Pixar Image Computer ability to render high-resolution three-dimensional color images. The computer division in 1986 was spun off as an independent business, with the help of the cofounder of Apple, Steve Jobs.

In 1989, Pixar began making computer-animated television commercials, and just a couple years after it agreed with Disney to jointly develop, produce, and distribute three feature-length animated motion pictures. To accommodate its new focus, Pixar began reorganizing and spent the next several years working on Toy Story, making it the first entirely computer-animated feature film. In 2006 Jobs sold Pixar to a larger company and Catmull was named president of both Walt Disney Animation Studios and Pixar Animation Studios and Lasseter became the studios’ chief creative officer.
